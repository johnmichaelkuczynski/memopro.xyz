1. Psychosis as a Mental Impairment Rooted in a Break with External Reality
Psychosis is a severe mental impairment characterized by a profound disconnection from external reality, where individuals experience distortions in perception and belief that prevent accurate interaction with the world. This break manifests as hallucinations—seeing or hearing things that are not present—or delusions, such as believing in conspiracies or false identities that have no basis in fact. For instance, a patient diagnosed with schizophrenia might hear voices commanding them to act, as documented in a 2019 study by the National Institute of Mental Health, which found that approximately 70% of schizophrenia patients experience auditory hallucinations at some point. This disconnection is not a mere lapse in judgment but a sustained state, often precipitated by genetic predispositions, trauma, or substance abuse, such as methamphetamine use, which a 2021 report in the Journal of Addiction Medicine linked to a 30% increased risk of psychotic episodes. The sustaining factor is the brain’s altered processing of sensory input, as shown in fMRI studies from Johns Hopkins University in 2018, which identified abnormal activity in the prefrontal cortex during psychotic states. Unlike temporary confusion, psychosis fundamentally alters one’s grasp of what is real, making it impossible to navigate everyday situations without intervention. This primary focus on external reality distinguishes psychosis from other impairments, as the patient’s internal emotional state, while affected, is not the central issue. Historical cases, like that of John Nash, the mathematician whose schizophrenia led to delusions of government surveillance (as detailed in his biography A Beautiful Mind), illustrate how reality loss overrides other mental functions. Thus, psychosis is not just a mental quirk but a debilitating condition where the external world becomes an unreliable construct, demanding clinical attention to restore functional awareness.

2. Neurosis as a Mental Impairment Rooted in a Break with Emotional Condition
Neurosis, in contrast, is a mental impairment defined by a disconnection from one’s own emotional condition, where individuals suppress or avoid their feelings to a degree that disrupts normal functioning. This manifests as excessive anxiety, obsessive thoughts, or phobias that stem from an inability to process emotions like fear, guilt, or anger healthily. Hence, neurosis centers on fleeing one’s inner emotional landscape, creating distress that, while severe, typically does not reach the reality-breaking threshold of psychosis.
