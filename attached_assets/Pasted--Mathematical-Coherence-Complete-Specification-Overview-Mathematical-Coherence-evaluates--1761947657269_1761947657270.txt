# Mathematical Coherence: Complete Specification

## Overview

Mathematical Coherence evaluates the logical validity of mathematical proofs, identifies errors, gaps, and invalid steps, and either repairs the proof orâ€”if the original theorem is unprovableâ€”finds the nearest provable theorem and provides a valid proof for it.

This is a specialized coherence type that applies formal logical rigor to mathematical arguments.

**IMPORTANT PLACEMENT:** Add this as an additional option within the Coherence Meter dropdown menu, labeled "Mathematical (Proof Validity)".

---

## Core Concept

Mathematical proofs require **perfect logical coherence**â€”every step must follow validly from previous steps or established axioms/theorems. Unlike argumentative writing where some looseness is acceptable, mathematical proofs must be **completely rigorous**.

This function:
1. **Analyzes** attempted proofs for logical validity
2. **Identifies** errors, gaps, unjustified steps, and invalid inferences
3. **Repairs** the proof if the theorem is correct but the proof is flawed
4. **Pivots** if the theorem is false: finds the nearest true theorem and proves that instead

---

## What Mathematical Coherence Checks

### 1. Logical Validity of Steps
- Does each step follow from previous steps?
- Are all inferences valid?
- Are there hidden assumptions?

### 2. Completeness
- Are there gaps in the argument?
- Are intermediate steps missing?
- Does the conclusion actually follow?

### 3. Rigor
- Are quantifiers used correctly?
- Are domain/range restrictions respected?
- Are special cases handled?

### 4. Correctness of Invoked Results
- Are cited theorems stated correctly?
- Do they actually apply in this context?
- Are hypotheses satisfied?

### 5. Overall Provability
- Is the claimed theorem actually true?
- If false, what's the nearest true statement?

---

## The Two Output Modes

### Mode A: Proof is Repairable
**When:** The theorem is correct but the proof has errors

**Output:**
1. Analysis of errors in original proof
2. Corrected proof with all gaps filled
3. Explanation of what was fixed

### Mode B: Theorem is False â†’ Find Nearest True Theorem
**When:** The claimed theorem cannot be proven because it's false

**Output:**
1. Explanation of why original theorem is false
2. Counterexample (if applicable)
3. Nearest provable theorem (weakened hypothesis, strengthened conclusion, or restricted domain)
4. Complete valid proof of the new theorem
5. Explanation of how new theorem relates to original attempt

## Success Criteria

Mathematical Coherence successfully validates proofs when it:

1. **Correctly identifies logical errors** in proof steps
2. **Recognizes when theorems are false** and provides counterexamples
3. **Generates valid proofs** with no gaps or invalid steps
4. **Finds sensible "nearest theorems"** when original is false
5. **Uses proper mathematical notation and rigor**
6. **Explains transformations clearly** so users learn from corrections

The function fails when it:
- Marks valid proofs as invalid
- Fails to catch logical errors
- Generates proofs with gaps or errors
- Suggests "nearest theorems" that are unrelated to original
- Uses informal or imprecise language
