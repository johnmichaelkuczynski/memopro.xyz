Logical dependence-relations hold among propositions, which are defined as statements that can be definitively evaluated as either true or false, such as "2 + 2 = 4" or "The sky is green." These relations determine how the truth of one proposition can imply or necessitate the truth of another, as seen in deductive arguments like modus ponens, where if "If A then B" and "A" are true, then "B" must also be true. Causal dependence-relations, on the other hand, hold among events, which are specific occurrences located in space and time, such as the eruption of Mount Vesuvius in 79 AD or a car accident on a specific date. These relations indicate how one event can bring about another, for instance, how a spark can cause a forest fire, as documented in the 2018 California wildfires where dry conditions and human error led to over 1.6 million acres burned (source: California Department of Forestry and Fire Protection). Some events clearly depend on prior events, forming a causal chain, like how a drought in 2017 contributed to the severity of those 2018 fires. Inferences, when legitimate, are made in accordance with established rules of inference, such as those codified in classical logic textbooks like Copi and Cohen's "Introduction to Logic." These rules, when legitimate, directly correspond to the underlying logical or causal dependence-relations, ensuring that conclusions drawn are valid reflections of reality, as demonstrated by the consistent application of syllogistic reasoning in legal arguments or scientific hypothesis testing. If a rule fails to mirror these relations, such as in fallacious reasoning like post hoc ergo propter hoc, the inference is invalid and does not hold.
